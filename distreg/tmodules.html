Mathematical Context

The Terwilliger Algebra (¥(T¥))

For a distance-regular graph ¥(¥Gamma¥), the Terwilliger algebra (or subconstituent algebra) ¥(T¥) is the finite-dimensional, non-commutative algebra generated by the adjacency matrix ¥(A¥) and the dual Bose-Mesner idempotents ¥(E_0^*, E_1^*, ¥dots, E_D^*¥).

The Dual Bose-Mesner Idempotents (¥(E_i^*¥))

For a fixed base vertex ¥(x¥), the vertices of the graph are partitioned into subconstituents based on their shortest-path distance ¥(i¥) from ¥(x¥). The dual Bose-Mesner idempotent ¥(E_i^*¥) is a diagonal projection matrix that "zeroes out" any values on vertices that are not at distance ¥(i¥) from ¥(x¥).

The Adjacency Matrix (¥(A¥))

The adjacency matrix ¥(A¥) represents the connections in the graph, where ¥(A_{uv} = 1¥) if vertices ¥(u¥) and ¥(v¥) are connected. When ¥(A¥) multiplies a column vector ¥(¥mathbf{v}¥), the value at vertex ¥(u¥) in the new vector ¥(A¥mathbf{v}¥) is exactly the sum of the values of all neighbors of ¥(u¥): ¥[ (A¥mathbf{v})_u = ¥sum_{v ¥sim u} ¥mathbf{v}_v ¥] This is why applying the action of ¥(A¥) in the visualization is exactly equivalent to replacing each node's value with the sum of its neighbors' values.

What is a ¥(T¥)-module?

A ¥(T¥)-module is a subspace ¥(W ¥subseteq ¥mathbb{C}^X¥) that is invariant under the action of the Terwilliger algebra. Specifically, this means that ¥(W¥) must be closed under multiplication by both the adjacency matrix (¥(AW ¥subseteq W¥)) and all dual idempotents (¥(E_i^* W ¥subseteq W¥)). An irreducible ¥(T¥)-module contains no non-zero proper ¥(T¥)-submodules.

Why only show the action of ¥(A¥)?

To rigorously verify a ¥(T¥)-module, we must check closure under ¥(A¥) and all ¥(E_i^*¥). However, the basis vectors ¥(¥mathbf{v}_i¥) presented here have been intentionally chosen so that each vector lies entirely within a single subconstituent of ¥(¥Gamma¥). Because they are already eigenvectors for the dual idempotents (applying ¥(E_i^*¥) returns the vector or zero), they are trivially closed under ¥(E_i^*¥). Therefore, to prove that the subspace is a ¥(T¥)-module, we only need to demonstrate closure under ¥(A¥).

The "Essential Part" of a Vector

Because each basis vector ¥(¥mathbf{v}_i¥) lies entirely within a single subconstituent, it contains many trivial zeroes outside of that subconstituent. We often represent the vector by its essential part. This is the vector obtained by deleting all entries corresponding to vertices that are not in that specific subconstituent. Hovering over a basis vector in the legend displays this condensed representation.
