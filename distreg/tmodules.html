About this Page

1. What is an irreducible ¥(T¥)-module?

Let ¥(¥Gamma = (X, ¥mathcal{E})¥) be a distance-regular graph. For a fixed base vertex ¥(x ¥in X¥), the vertices are partitioned into subconstituents ¥(¥Gamma_i¥) based on their shortest-path distance ¥(i¥) from ¥(x¥).

The Terwilliger algebra (or subconstituent algebra) ¥(T = T(x)¥) is the finite-dimensional, non-commutative algebra generated by the adjacency matrix ¥(A¥) and the dual Bose-Mesner idempotents ¥(¥{E_i^* ¥mid 0 ¥le i ¥le D¥}¥). The matrix ¥(E_i^*¥) is a diagonal projection matrix that "zeroes out" any values on vertices not in ¥(¥Gamma_i¥).

A ¥(T¥)-module is a subspace ¥(W ¥subseteq ¥mathbb{C}^X¥) that is invariant under the action of the Terwilliger algebra (i.e., closed under multiplication by ¥(A¥) and all ¥(E_i^*¥)). A ¥(T¥)-module is called irreducible if it contains no non-zero proper ¥(T¥)-submodules.

2. Where do these ¥(T¥)-modules come from?

These modules were derived as part of "Irreducible ¥(T¥)-modules of Distance-Regular Graphs with Valency Three," a paper and project from the Madison Experimental Mathematics (MXM) Lab at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, headed by Professor Paul Terwilliger.

The goal of the project was to find an orthogonal direct sum decomposition of the standard module ¥(V = ¥mathbb{C}^X¥) into irreducible ¥(T¥)-modules. The specific ¥(T¥)-modules featured here were found with the assistance of computational software such as R and Wolfram Mathematica.

3. How does this website prove these are irreducible ¥(T¥)-modules?

Using the Visualization:
Select a Graph and ¥(T¥)-module to load its topology and basis vectors. Pick an initial basis vector ¥(¥mathbf{v}_i¥) and click "Apply A" (or press Enter / 'A') to visualize the action of the adjacency matrix. The visualization demonstrates that applying ¥(A¥) yields a state that remains a perfect linear combination of the module's basis, proving closure under ¥(A¥). To prove closure under ¥(¥{E_i^*¥mid 0 ¥le i ¥le D¥}¥), we simply note that all vectors discussed henceforth are contained within a single subconstituent.

Essential Part of a Vector:
To that end, because each basis vector ¥(¥mathbf{v}_i¥) lies entirely within a single subconstituent, it contains many trivial zeroes outside of that subconstituent. We often represent the vector by its essential part. This is the vector obtained by deleting all entries corresponding to vertices that are not in that specific subconstituent. Hovering over a basis vector in the legend displays this condensed representation, with the number overset giving the subconstituent the vector is contained in.

Proof of Irreducibility:
Irreducibility is formally proven by Theorems 2 and 4 in the paper:

  • Theorem 2: For a distance-regular graph ¥(¥Gamma¥) with fixed vertex ¥(x¥) and Terwilliger algebra ¥(T=T(x)¥), there exists a unique primary (endpoint 0) irreducible ¥(T¥)-module ¥(W_0 ¥subseteq V = ¥mathbb{C}^{|X|}¥) defined as the span of the main diagonals of the nonzero ¥(E_i^*=E_i^*(x)¥) matrices.
  • Theorem 4: Let ¥(T=T(x)¥) be the Terwilliger algebra of a distance-regular graph ¥(¥Gamma = (X,¥mathcal{E})¥) with a fixed vertex ¥(x ¥in X¥). Let ¥(W¥) be a ¥(T¥)-module with endpoint ¥(¥alpha¥) such that ¥(¥dim(E_¥alpha^* W) = 1¥), with ¥(E_¥alpha^* W = ¥mathrm{span}¥{v_¥alpha¥}¥). If ¥(W¥) is generated by ¥(v_¥alpha¥) (i.e., ¥(W = T v_¥alpha¥)), then ¥(W¥) is irreducible.
