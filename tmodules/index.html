About this Page

1. What is an irreducible \(T\)-module?

Let \(\Gamma = (X, \mathcal{E})\) be a finite undirected distance-regular graph, where \(X\) is the vertex set and \(\mathcal{E}\) is the set of edges. For a fixed base vertex \(x \in X\), the vertices \(y \in X\) are partitioned into subconstituents \(\{\Gamma_i\}_{i=0}^D\) based on their shortest-path distance \(d(y,x)=i\) from \(x\), where \(D = \max\{d(y,z) \mid y,z \in X\}\).

The Terwilliger algebra (or subconstituent algebra) \(T = T(x)\) is the finite-dimensional, non-commutative algebra generated by the adjacency matrix \(A\) (where \((A)_{a,b}=1\) if \(\{a,b\} \in \mathcal{E}\) and 0 otherwise) and the dual primitive idempotents \(\{E_i^*\}_{i=0}^D\). The matrix \(E_i^*=E_i^*(x)\) is a diagonal projection matrix that "zeroes out" any values on vertices not in \(\Gamma_i\). In other words, for fixed \(x\), \({(E_i^*)}_{y,y}=1\) if \(d(y,x)=i\), and 0 otherwise.

A \(T\)-module is a subspace \(W \subseteq \mathbb{C}^X\) that is invariant under the action of the Terwilliger algebra (i.e., closed under multiplication by \(A\) and all \(E_i^*\)). A \(T\)-module is called irreducible if it does not properly contain any non-zero \(T\)-modules.

2. Where do these \(\text{ }T\)-modules come from?

These modules were derived as part of "Irreducible \(T\)-modules of Distance-Regular Graphs with Valency Three," a paper and project from the Madison Experimental Mathematics (MXM) Lab at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, headed by Professor Paul Terwilliger. The graduate mentor for the project was Jimmy Vineyard, and current and past undergraduate members of the group include Emi Bell, Kevin Kauflin, Barnabás Valkó, and Hanyi Wu.

It is known that there are exactly 13 distance-regular graphs of valency three. The paper gives, for each of these 13 graphs, an orthogonal direct-sum decomposition of the standard module \(V = \mathbb{C}^X\) into irreducible \(T\)-modules, with an explicit basis for each.

3. How does the visualization work?

Select a graph and a \(T\)-module, then choose an initial basis vector \(\mathbf{v}_i\). Clicking "Apply \(A\)" updates each vertex's value to the sum of its neighbors' current values, which is exactly the action of the adjacency matrix. The visualization shows that the result stays within the span of the module's basis, demonstrating closure under \(A\). Closure under each \(E_i^*\) holds automatically, since every basis vector is supported on a single subconstituent.

Because basis vectors contain many trivial zeros outside their subconstituent, hovering over a vector in the legend displays its essential part: the restriction to its subconstituent shown as a row vector, with the number overset indicating which one.

Irreducibility of the non-primary modules follows from the fact that each is generated by a single seed vector \(\nu\) with \(\dim(E_\alpha^* W) = 1\), as established in Theorem 4 of the paper. The unique primary module (endpoint 0) is handled separately by Theorem 1.

4. What interactive features are available?

Color Mapping: Throughout this simulation, distinct colors are uniquely assigned to each basis vector. If a vertex currently has a non-zero contributions from a basis vector, it is colored with respect to that basis vector. These colors are used consistently across the "Current Linear Combination" equation, the matrix representing \(A\), and the graph vertices to indicate active components. If a vertex's current value is a linear combination of multiple non-zero contributions from basis vectors, its node will split into multiple colors to visually represent each distinct contributing vector. Note: This coloring scheme differs completely from the graph colorings in the original paper, which were used strictly to distinguish subconstituents.

Graph Tooltips: Hover over any vertex to see its current total value, a history of scalar contributions flowing in from past neighbors, and a detailed breakdown of the specific basis vector components making up that value.

Subconstituent Highlights: Mouse over or click a row in the table of subconstituents (\(\Gamma_i\)) to highlight that subconstituent on the graph. Clear by using the "Reset Highlights" button.

Action of A: The matrix representing the action of \(A\) on the selected basis is displayed in the bottom right. The column corresponding to \(A \mathbf{v}_i\) is highlighted when \(A\) is applied to specific basis vector \(\mathbf{v}_i\).

Vector Legend: Hover over a basis vector in the legend to view its "essential part," which isolates its non-zero values to its specific subconstituent. Vectors can be chosen by clicking in the legend.

Animation Controls: Click "Animation Settings" on the left panel to slow down, speed up, or eliminate the action animations at any time.

Data Downloads: Use the buttons in the top left of the graph viewport to download the Adjacency Matrix (\(A\)) as a text file, or download a ZIP file of the dual Bose-Mesner idempotents (\(\{E_i^*\}_0^D\)) for the selected graph. Alternatively, click the green download button in the top right to download all matrices for all graphs (.zip, ~645KB)
